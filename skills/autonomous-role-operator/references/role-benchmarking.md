# Role Benchmarking

Use this reference when the user asks what excellent performance should look like for their position.

## Benchmark Source Selection

Prefer sources in this order:

- Internal role expectations, performance frameworks, strategy docs, OKRs, scorecards, job ladders, and manager feedback.
- Authoritative public role frameworks from respected organizations.
- Current industry benchmarks, standards, or professional bodies.
- High-quality practitioner writing from credible operators in the same domain.
- Web research for current role-specific trends when the field changes quickly.

Use web browsing for current or fast-changing fields. Include source links in benchmark docs.

## Generic Benchmark Dimensions

Adapt these dimensions to the user's role:

- Strategy and direction.
- Operating system and cadence.
- Stakeholder trust and communication.
- Decision quality and decision velocity.
- Execution and follow-through.
- Risk management.
- Talent, delegation, and leverage.
- Customer/user/business impact.
- Craft, technical, clinical, creative, or professional excellence, depending on role.
- Tooling, automation, AI leverage, and system improvement.
- Ethics, compliance, safety, and confidentiality.

## Conversion Rule

Do not stop at advice. Convert every benchmark into an operating mechanism:

- What artifact should exist?
- Who owns it?
- How often is it reviewed?
- What source data feeds it?
- What decision does it support?
- What success signal proves it works?
- What guardrail prevents misuse?
- When should it stop?

## Role-Agnostic Language

Avoid hard-coded engineering terms unless the user is actually in engineering.

Use:

- `workstream` instead of `initiative` when generic.
- `stakeholder` instead of `executive` unless executives are involved.
- `professional risk` instead of `technical risk` unless technical risk exists.
- `quality gate` instead of `launch gate` unless the role ships products.
- `operating brief` instead of `engineering narrative` unless engineering context is present.

## Current-Potential Model

For every role, model both:

- Current role: what the user is actually accountable for now.
- Possible role: what the user could become if they maximized leverage, trust, scope, craft, and strategic impact in their environment.

The possible role should be ambitious but grounded in evidence from current responsibilities and context.
